7 Sampling

In this chapter we will introduce new concepts that will serve as the basis for the remainder of the text: sampling. We will see that the tools that you learned in the Data Exploration portion of this book (data visualization, “tidy” data format, and data wrangling) will also play an important role here. As mentioned before, the concepts throughout this text all build into a culmination allowing you to create better stories with data.

We begin with some helpful definitions related to sampling that form the theoretical underpinnings of statical inference, which we will cover in the upcoming “Hypothesis Testing” and “Confidence Intervals”, and when we revisit regression.

7.2 Definitions

  1. Observational unit
  2. Population
  3. Population parameter
  4. Sample
  5. Sampling
  6. Representative sampling
  7. Generalizability
  8. Bias
  9. Statistic
  10. Point estimates/sample statistics
  11. Census

Moral:

  • We use sampling when it is too expensive/tiring to conduct a census. Thus, we need to guess at the value of the population parameter using a point estimate based on a sample of size \(n\).
  • When sampling is done at random, or in other words in an unbiased fashion, the sample is representative of the population
  • When the sample is representative of the population, then any results based on the sample generalize to the entire population.
  • When any results based on the sample generalize to the entire population, the point estimate/sample statistic is a “good” guess of the population parameter.

7.3 Representative sampling

Do the following

  • Identify the population of interest and what you want to know about them.
  • Comment on the representativeness of the sampling procedure

for the following 5 scenarios:

  1. You go to the homepages of CNN and Fox News, and see the results of online polls they both conducted directly on their website on attitudes towards gun control.
  2. The Royal Air Force wants to study how resistant their airplanes are to bullets. They study the bullet holes on all the airplanes on the tarmac after an air battle against the Luftwaffe (German Air Force).
  3. You want to know the average income of graduates of your academic institution in the last 10 years. So you get the records of 10 randomly chosen alumni. They all answer and you take the average.
  4. Imagine it’s 1993 i.e. almost all households have landlines. You want to know the average number of people in each household in your hometown. You randomly pick out 500 phone numbers from the phone book and conduct a phone survey.
  5. You want to know the prevalence of illegal downloading of TV shows among students at an academic institution. You get the emails of 100 randomly chosen students and ask them “How many times did you download a pirated TV show last week?”
