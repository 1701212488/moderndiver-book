2 Is this textbook for you?

Principles of this book

  1. Blurring the lines between lecture and lab
  2. Focus on the entire data/science research pipeline
  3. It’s all about data, data, data
  4. Use simulation/resampling in intro stats, not mathematical probability/large sample approximation theory
  5. Don’t fence off the “programming” pool; throw them in! (With flotation aids of course)
  6. Complete reproducibility
  7. Ultimately the best textbook is one you’ve written yourself

Blurring the lines between lecture and lab With the rise of laptops, we can break down the dichotomies/blur the lines between: Lab vs lecture, Computing vs math Treatment of material in lecture vs treatment of material in textbook Inverted/flipped classroom. Not about video lecture, but how you leverage the fact that everyone is together Active learning: 10-15 lecturing to prime thinking, but then they do their own thing at their own pace Focus on the entire data/science research pipeline Wickham and Grolemund graphic Cobb: Minimize prerequisites to research. Answering questions with data. It’s all about data, data, data: Reorganization: First half of the class is all about data, then traditional intro stats topics. R packages for data: Large, complex, rich and yet still in a standardized format that’s easy to load format. An ideal balance. Data manipulation: Game changer: dplyr. The verb describing the action you want to perform on your data set is the command name. Data visualization: “You can’t teach ggplot in intro stats” We feel the way David Robinson feels: teach ggplot2 to beginners. They don’t need to unlearn anything. Key is spending time unpacking the Grammar of Graphics and limiting the scope to the 5NG, in increasing order of complexity i.e. geom_bar(stat=”identity”). Need analogy to describe our 5NG approach: Sandbox? Training wheels? Mother robin regurgitating worms for the baby robins? Use simulation and resampling, not mathematical probability and large sample approximation theory: Conceptual understanding (GAISE) of sampling distribution and standard errors. Frees up space by downweighting mathematical approaches to probability and long build up to central limit theorem, allowing us more time for data. mosaic package do(), rflip, resample() functions. While we’re not afraid to get into the weeds of data manipulation/visualization, but because there is too much going on when trying to teach statistical inference, in this case we argue you must absolutely minimize the gap between what and the how. Don’t fence off the “programming” pool; throw them in! (With flotation aids of course) Instead of shielding students from algorithmic thinking and computational logic, we jump in head first. Not programming per se: no indexing or for loops. Rather teach them enough just enough to get the job done. dplyr and mosaic are huge for this They’re going to need programming in so many other situations, so why don’t we take the lead? Idea that computers are stupid is a tough one for novices to shed: creating variables before using them, parentheses have to match Encourage collaborative learning Complete reproducibility (Might be able to merge with 7 below, not sure) Right down to the axes labels and sentence punctuation via RStudio bookdown Encourage use of R Markdown We always found it frustrating when textbooks give examples but not the source code. We give you the source code Ultimately the best textbook is one you’ve written yourself Complete customizability: Only you know your audience, your skills, your priorities.
